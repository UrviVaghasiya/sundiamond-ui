Diamond Carat

The heavier, the higher the value:

Carat is a word that refers to the weight of a diamond. Prior to the 20th century, diamonds were measured using carob seeds, which were small and uniform and served as a perfect counterweight to the diamond. The word "carob” isthe origin of the word "carat" that we use now.

The diamond size is proportional to its carat weight. When rough diamonds are cut and polished into finished diamonds, up to 55 to 65% of the total carat weight may be lost.

Diamond Color

The whiter, the higher the value:

Diamond color can be graded on a scale from D-Z and is divided into five broad categories (colorless, near colorless, faint, very light and light). All Diamonds come in all colors of the spectrum. The predominant color you see in a diamond which is yellow, caused by the trace element nitrogen.

Generally, when comparing color between two diamonds, the diamonds need to be at least one or two color grades apart to even begin to see a difference. As you can see from the below images, when diamonds are in the face-up position it is almost impossible to see any color. When you see the diamond from the side profile, you may start to detect some color; however, diamonds are admired for their beauty from the face-up position and not the side.

Diamond Clarity

The fewer flaws, the higher the value:

Clarity refers to how the diamonds are clean or clear with respect to natural microscopic characteristics that were trapped within or on the diamond while it was forming. Internal characteristics are called inclusions, and characteristics on the surface of the Diamond are known as blemishes. Inclusions can be crystals of a foreign material or structural imperfections such as tiny cracks, known as feathers, which can appear whitish or cloudy.

Often times the inclusions are microscopic diamonds that were absorbed by the larger crystal before the diamond was brought out to the surface of the Earth. The quantity, size, color, orientation, location, and inclusion's visibility all affect the final clarity grade of a diamond. Diamonds with no or few inclusions are considered particularly highly valued and rare.

Diamond Cut

The more sparkle, the higher the value:

The cut of a diamond doesn't refer to the diamond's shape, it also refers to how effectively the diamond returns light back to the viewer's eye. A diamond with a well-cut will appear very brilliant and fiery, while a diamond with a poor cut can appear dark and lifeless, regardless of its color or clarity.

Not only do well-cut diamonds appear more brilliant, but they also tend to appear larger than other diamonds of the same carat weight. An "ideal" diamond has increased brilliance and diameter relative to more deeply-cut diamonds.
